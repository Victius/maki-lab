1.5 Normal Subgroup and Quotient Group

Normal Subgroup

​ In section 1.3, we know a group $G$ have such a partition $$ G = \bigcup_{g\in R} Hg $$ if $H\leqslant G$. Can we use binary operation between these cosets? In general, $Hx\cdot Hy$ is just itself. It can not equal to other simple things. But, if we have a more condition $\forall g \in G, gH=Hg$, then we have $$ Hx\cdot Hy=H(Hx)y=H(xy) $$ which is also a coset of $H$. This fact appeal to us to notice such a special subgroup, which is commutative with all elements in group $G$.

Definition 5.1 Normal subgroup, $N\lhd G$.

​ If $N$ is a subgroup of $G$, and also satisfied $$ \forall g\in G, gA=Ag $$ then we called $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, which is denoted by $N\lhd G$.

Example 1 For all $n\in\Z$, $n\Z\lhd \Z$. This is true because for all $m\in\Z$, $$ \begin{align} m + n\Z &= {m+nk|\ k\in\Z} \ &= {nk+m|\ k\in\Z} \ &= n\Z + m \end{align} $$ Ex 1.5.1 All subgroup of an Abelian group is a normal subgroup.

​ We have and some interesting result about normal subgroup.

Theorem 5.2 'a subgroup which order is two' ​ $N$ is a subgroup of $G$. If the order of $N$ equals to two, then $N$ is a normal subgroup.

​ Proof: If $g\in G$ is not in $N$, then $$ G = N\cup gN = N\cup Ng \quad\text{(Why?)} $$ so $gN=Ng$.

Theorem 5.3 some properties of normal subgroup ​ Assume $N$ is a subgroup of $G$, then TFAE: ​ (1) $N\lhd G$ ​ (2) $\forall g\in G, gNg^{-1}=N$ (i.e. $N$ is conjugate with itself.) ​ (3) $\forall g\in G, gNg^{-1}\subset N$ ​ (4) $N_G(N)=G$ ​ (5) every left coset of $N$ is right coset.

​ Proof: ​ (1)$\iff$(2): the definition of normal subgroup. ​ (2)$\iff$(3): (2)$\to$(3) is obviously. We only need to prove (3)$\to$(2). ​ If $\forall g\in G, gNg^{-1}\subset N$, then $$ \begin{align} &\forall g\in G: gNg^{-1}\subset N \ \therefore\ &\forall g\in G: N\subset g^{-1}Ng \ \therefore\ &\forall h\in G: N\subset hNh^{-1} &\text{(Let $h=g^{-1}$,and $G=G^{-1}$)}\ \therefore\ &\forall g\in G: N\subset gNg^{-1} &\text{(substitute the 'dummy' variable)} \end{align} $$ so $\forall g\in G, gNg^{-1}=N$. ​ (2)$\iff$(4): the definition of normalizer. ​ (1)$\iff$(5): (1)$\to$(5) is obviously. We only need to prove (5)$\to$(1). ​ Because of (5), for $g\in G$, there is a $g'\in G$ s.t. $gN=Ng'$. And then $g=ge\in gN=Ng'$, thus $Ng=Ng'=gN$.

Ex 1.5.2 [!] let $G={(a,b)|a\in\mathbb{R}_*,b\in\mathbb{R}}$, define 'multiplication' $$ (a,b)(c,d)=(ac,ad+b) $$ Let $K={(1,b)|b\in\mathbb{R}}$. Try to prove that: $K\lhd G$.

Ex 1.5.3 Let $N < M < G$. ​ (1) Try to prove that: if $N\lhd G$, then $N\lhd M$. ​ (2) If $N\lhd M$, then do we have the conclusion: $N\lhd G$?

Ex 1.5.4 [*,!] Let $M,N$ are normal subgroups of $G$, if $M\cap N={e}$, then prove that for all $m\in M$ and $n\in N$, $mn=nm$.

Quotient group

​ From the discussion above, we seem to say that if $N$ is a normal subgroup of group $G$, then all right (or left, which is actually the same. In order to keep simple, we just called them cosets.) cosets of $N$ form a group. Furthermore, some people may give such a proof:

​ 'Proof': Let $\mathcal{A}$ is the collection of all cosets of $N$, then we have

| $\forall Na,Nb\in\mathcal{A}, Na\cdot Nb\in\mathcal{A}$ | | ------------------------------------------------------------ | | $\forall Na,Nb,Nc\in\mathcal{A}, (Na\cdot Nb)Nc=Na(Nb\cdot Nc)$ | | $\exist N\in\mathcal{A},\forall Na\in\mathcal{A},Na\cdot N=N\cdot Na=Na$ | | $\forall Na\in\mathcal{A},\exist Na^{-1}\in\mathcal{A},Na^{-1}\cdot Na=Na\cdot Na^{-1}=N$ |

​ Do you think it is a perfect proof? Here the writer has a question: if elements $Na,Nb\in\mathcal{A}$, which element in $\mathcal{A}$ is equal to $Na\cdot Nb$? Some self-confident people give the writer a definite answer: that's easy! $Na\cdot Nb = N(ab)$. ​ OK, now questions continue to appear. We have already know that the order of the coset $Na$ is equal to the order of $N$, that is to say, in general, $N$ is not a one-element set, and we can find a $a'\neq a$ in $Na$. If $a'\in Na$, then $Na'=Na$, because $a'a^{-1}\in N$ (and use 3.11). So we have a deduction below: if $Na\cdot Nb = N(ab)$ is right, then we have $$ N(a'b) = Na'\cdot Nb = Na \cdot Nb = N(ab) $$ ​ We finally get a conclusion: if $Na=Na'$, then $N(a'b)=N(ab)$, which is not obvious. This is where the problem lies. It is a good thing that this conclusion in here is right (which we will prove below), but we cannot assure we always have such fortune. So we first prove this, then we keep to discuss this problem.

Theorem 5.4 'well-defined' of multiplication of cosets Assume $N$ is a normal subgroup of group $G$. $a,a',b$ are in $G$. If cosets $Na$ equals to $Na'$, then $N(ab)=N(a'b)$.

​ Proof: we wts. $(ab)(a'b)^{-1}\in N$, which is obviously right. ($aa'^{-1}\in N$).

​ Just like the sub-title of 5.4, we usually called this type of problem 'well-defined'. What does it means? At the beginning of this course, we have introduced the binary operation, which has the form of a map: $$ f:A\times B\to C,\ (a,b)\mapsto f(a,b) $$ Back to our problem, the binary operation is $$ \cdot: \mathcal{A}\times \mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{A}, (Na,Nb)\mapsto N(ab) $$ If we have $a'\neq a$ which leads to $Na=Na'$, then we must guarantee that $N(a'b)=N(ab)$. Otherwise, one 'input' $(Na,Nb)=(Na',Nb)$ will get two (or even more) 'output' $N(ab)$ and $N(a'b)$, thus '$\cdot$' cannot be a map, which is not 'well-defined'. ​ So, from now on, if we need to deal with some questions which have to define a 'map', then we will always consider whether this map is 'well-defined'.

​ When we have the theorem 4.4, we can definitely to claim that

Definition 5.5 the quotient/ factor group, $G/N$ ​ If $N$ is a normal subgroup of group $G$, then all cosets of $N$ form a group, which we called it the quotient group, $G/N$ (read $G$ modulo $N$, or simply $G$ mod $N$). ​ The element in $G/N$, $gN$ or $Ng$, we usually write it as $\bar g$ without confusion.

Example 2 In example 1, we have proved $n\Z\lhd \Z$ for all $n\in \Z$. In particular, choose $n>2$, then we have the quotient group: $$ \Z/n\Z = {\bar0, \bar1, \cdots, \overline{n-1}} $$ Can you find the relationship between this group to 'the group of roots of $x^n=1$ in $\C$' (section 1.2)?

Theorem 5.6 the order of the quotient group ​ If $N$ is a normal subgroup of group $G$, then the order of $G/N$ equals to $|G|/|N|$. [Ex 1.5.5]

​ Until now, we have already learn many 'quotient' things in math, such as quotient set, quotient topology, quotient space (in linear algebra), and so on. Why all these different concepts have the same adjective 'quotient'? In general, the 'quotient' something implies a partition of it.

Example 3 quotient operation vs. quotient group ​ (1) $6\div3 \to$ 6 books, 3 of them consists of a packet, and you have 2 packets. ​ (2) $G/N \to$ $|G|$ books, $|N|$ of them consists of a packet, and you have $|G|/|N|$ packets. (Notice that in group theory, all cosets of one subgroup have the same order, so we can do such an analogy.)
