The fundamental theorem of homomorphisms

​ A group without the 'dimension' like the linear space, so every conclusion with respect to the dimension in linear algebra has not the parallel conclusion in group theory. But others are not the same. To be a parallel conclusion with the part of Theorem L.3 (2), we have:

Theorem 7.1 the fundamental theorem of homomorphisms/ the first isomorphism theorem Let $f:G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then there is an isomorphism $\bar f$ induced by $f$: $$ \begin{align} \bar f: G/\ker f &\xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{im} f \ \bar g &\mapsto f(g) \end{align} $$

​ Proof: Define the 'map': $$ \begin{align} \bar f: G/\ker f &\to \mathrm{im} f \ \bar g &\mapsto f(g) \end{align} $$ ​ (i) wts. $\bar f$ is well-defined. (Why we need to prove it? What is the actually we need to prove?) ​ Let $a,b\in G$ and $\bar a = \bar b$, then we have $$ \begin{align} \because &\ \bar a=\bar b \ \therefore &\ (\ker f)a = (\ker f)b \ \therefore &\ (\ker f)(ab^{-1}) = \ker f \ \therefore &\ ab^{-1} \in\ker f &\text{(3.9)} \ \therefore &\ f(ab^{-1}) = e' \ \therefore &\ f(a)f(b^{-1}) = e' &\text{(6.1)}\ \therefore &\ f(a)(f(b))^{-1} = e' &\text{(6.3 (2))} \ \therefore &\ f(a) = f(b) \end{align} $$ So $\bar f$ is well-defined. ​ (ii) wts. $\bar f$ is a homomorphism. ​ For all $\bar a, \bar b \in G/\ker f$, we have $$ \begin{align} \bar f(\bar a)\bar f(\bar b) &= f(a)f(b) \ &= f(ab) &\text{(6.1)} \ &= \bar f(\overline{ab}) \ &= \bar f(\bar a\bar b) &\text{(Don't forget this step!)} \end{align} $$ So $\bar f$ is a homomorphism. ​ (iii) wts. $\bar f$ is an injection. ​ For all $\bar a, \bar b \in G/\ker f$ ,we have $$ \begin{align} \bar f(\bar a) = \bar f(\bar b) &\to f(a) = f(b) \ &\to f(a)(f(b))^{-1} = e' \ &\to f(a)f(b^{-1}) = e' &\text{(6.3 (2))}\ &\to f(ab^{-1}) = e' &\text{(6.1)}\ &\to ab^{-1} \in\ker f \ &\to (\ker f)(ab^{-1}) = \ker f &\text{(3.9)}\ &\to \bar a = \bar b \end{align} $$ Thus $\bar f$ is a injection. ​ (iv) wts. $\bar f$ is a surjection. ​ For all $x\in \mathrm{im} f$, there is a $a\in G$ s.t. $f(a) = x$. Thus $x = f(a)= \bar f(\bar a)\in\mathrm{im}\bar f$, i.e. $\mathrm{im} f\subset \mathrm{im} \bar f$. And use (2), $\mathrm{im}\bar f\subset \mathrm{im}f$. So $\mathrm{im}\bar f = \mathrm{im}f$, and $\bar f$ is a surjection. ​ So, $\bar f$ is a homomorphism and a bijection. That is to say, $\bar f$ is an isomorphism.

The decomposition of a homomorphism

​ We have known that if $f:G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then $\bar f: G/\ker f \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{im} f, \bar g \mapsto f(g)$ is an isomorphism. Can we link these two homomorphisms?
​ Firstly we can establish homomorphisms from $G$ to $G/\ker f$, and $\mathbb{im}f$ to $H$. There are such 'natural' maps: $$ \begin{align} \pi: G &\to G/\ker f \ g &\mapsto (\ker f)g \end{align} $$

$$ \begin{align} i:\mathbb{im}f &\to H \ f(g) &\mapsto f(g) \end{align} $$

which $\pi$ is a surjective homomorphism, and $i$ is an embedding homomorphism. [Ex 1.7.3] ​ Now we express all these maps using a map graph.

Some isomorphism theorems

​ We will give some 'isomorphism' theorems as applications of the fundamental theorem.

Theorem 7.2 diamond isomorphism theorem/ the second ~ ​ Let $N\lhd G,\ H\leqslant G$, then ​ (1) $H\cap N \lhd H$ ​ (2) $N\lhd NH \leqslant G$ ​ (3) $NH/N\cong H/(H\cap N)$

​ Analysis: For (1) and (2), we do not need any more analysis, just do it normally. ​ The third proposition has such a significant form, which tells us to use the fundamental theorem. We have two ways to prove that, for both left and right side are quotient groups.

​ Proof: ​ (1) $H\cap N$ is obviously a subgroup of $H$ (theorem 3.5), and for any $h\in H$, $h(H\cap N)=hH\cap hN$ (Why?). Because $hH=H=Hh$ and $hN=Nh$, we have $hH\cap hN=Hh\cap Nh=(H\cap N)h$. Thus $H\cap N \lhd H$. ​ (2) We only need to prove that $NH$ is a subgroup of $G$, (The reader can use ex 1.3.5 directly.) because $N$ is obviously a subset of $NH$. $$ \begin{align} NH(NH)^{-1} &= NHH^{-1}N^{-1} &\text{(Why?)} \ &= NHN &\text{($H,N$ are subgroups.)} \ &= NNH &\text{($N$ is normal.)} \ &= NH &\text{(Why?)} \end{align} $$ That is to say, $NH\leqslant G$. ​ (3) Define a map $$ \begin{align} f: NH &\to H/(H\cap N) \ nh &\mapsto h(H\cap N) \end{align} $$ This is a homomorphism because $$ \begin{align} f(n1h1)f(n2h2) &= h1(N\cap H)\cdot h2(N\cap H) \ &= h1h2(N\cap H) \end{align} $$ and $$ \begin{align} f(n1h1\cdot n2h2) &= f(n1n2\cdot h1h2) \ &= h1h2(N\cap H) \ &= f(n1h1)f(n2h2) \end{align} $$ ​ Now we will induce the $\ker f$. $$ \begin{align} \ker f &= {nh\in NH|\ f(nh)=e'} \ &= {nh\in NH|\ h(H\cap N)=H\cap N} \ &= {nh\in NH|\ h\in H\cap N} \ \end{align} $$ Actually we have $h\in H\cap N \to nh \in N(H\cap N)=N$ because of (1) and $nh\in N \to h\in N\to n\in (H\cap N)$. So $$ \ker f={nh\in NH|\ nh\in N} = {nh|\ nh\in N} = N $$ Finally we use theorem 7.1 and prove the proposition (3). ​ The other way, $g:H\to NH/N$, is easier than before. The writer would like to leave it to the reader. [Ex 1.7.4]

Theorem 7.3 the third isomorphism theorem ​ Let $N\lhd G, M\lhd G$ and $N\leqslant M$, then $G/M\cong(G/N)/(M/N)$.

​ Analysis: Here both left and right side are quotient groups, but use the map $G/N\to G/M$ is more easier than $G\to (G/N)/(M/N)$. So the former are left to the reader. [Ex 1.7.6] ​ The latter is very interesting. We need to prove $M/N \lhd G/N$ and understand the form of elements in $(G/N)/(M/N)$, which is $gN(M/N)$. The more details will be discussed after this proof.

​ Proof: (i) wts. $M/N \lhd G/N$. ​ It is easy to say that $M/N$ is a subgroup of $G/N$, and for every $gN\in G/N$, we have $$ \begin{align} gN(M/N) &= {gNmN|\ m\in M} \ &= {gmN |\ m\in M} \ \end{align} $$ Because $M\lhd G$, for all $g\in G$, $gM=Mg$. That is to say, for all $m\in M$, there is a $m'\in M$ s.t. $gm=m'g$, so we can continue to deduce $$ \begin{align} gN(M/N) &= {gmN |\ m\in M} \ &\subset {m'gN| m'\in M} &\text{(not "="!)} \ &= {m'gNN| m'\in M} \ &= {m'NgN| m'\in M} \ &= (M/N)gN \end{align} $$ Thus $gN(M/N)(gN)^{-1}\subset (M/N)$, i.e. $M/N\lhd G/N$ (theorem 5.3). ​ (ii) wts. $f:G\to (G/N)/(M/N)$ is a surjective homomorphism, and $\ker f=M$. ​ Define a map $$ \begin{align} f:G &\to (G/N)/(M/N) \ g &\mapsto gN(M/N) \end{align} $$ then we have $$ \begin{align} f(g1)f(g2) &= g1N(M/N)\cdot g2N(M/N) \ &= g1N(g2N(M/N))(M/N) &\text{(use (i))} \ &= g1g2N(M/N) \ &= f(g1g2) \end{align} $$ and $f$ is obviously a surjection. ​ For the kernel of $f$ $$ \begin{align} \ker f &= {g\in G|\ f(g)=e'} \ &= {g\in G|\ (gN)(M/N) = M/N} \ &= {g\in G|\ gN\in M/N} \ &= {g\in G|\ g\in M} &\text{(Why?)}\ &= G\cap M \ &= M \end{align} $$ That is what we want to prove.

​ Here we want to discuss more things. First, what is the form of elements in $(G/N)/(M/N)$? We just need to write it step by step $$ \begin{align} (G/N)/(M/N) &= {x(M/N)|\ x\in G/N} \ &= {gN(M/N)|\ g\in G} \ \end{align} $$ Second, can we simplify the form of $N(M/N)$? Also do it step by step $$ \begin{align} N(M/N) &= {nmN|\ n\in N, m\in M} \ &= {(nm)N|\ n\in N, m\in M} \ &= (NM)/N &\text{($N\lhd M$, so $MN=M$ is a group.)} \ &= M/N \end{align} $$ In conclusion, we can get $$ \begin{align} (G/N)/(M/N) &= {gN(M/N)|\ g\in G} \ &= {g(M/N)|\ g\in G} \ \end{align} $$ For every $g(M/N)$, $$ \begin{align} g(M/N) &= {gmN|\ m\in M} \ &= {gNm|\ m\in M} \ &= (gN)\cdot{m\in M} \ &= gNM \ &= gM \end{align} $$ So $$ \begin{align} (G/N)/(M/N) &= {g(M/N)|\ g\in G} \ &= {gM|\ g\in G} \ &= G/M \end{align} $$ ??? Theorem 7.4 Lattice isomorphism theorem/ the fourth ~ [Ex 1.7.7] ​ Let $N$ is a normal subgroup of the group $G$. Assume two set collections $S$ and $S'$ $$ \begin{align} S &= {A\leqslant G|\ A\geqslant N} &\text{(all subgroups of $G$ which contains $N$)} \ S' &= {A'\leqslant G/N} &\text{(all subgroups of $G/N$)}\ \end{align} $$ Then there is a bijection between two collections $$ \begin{align} S &\cong S' \ A &\mapsto A/N \end{align} $$ and it has the following properties: for all $A,B\leqslant G$ with $N\leqslant A$ and $N\leqslant B$ ​ (1) $A\leqslant B \iff A/N\leqslant B/N$. ​ (2) $A\lhd G \iff A/N\lhd G/N$. ​ (3) $(A\cap B)/N = (A/N\cap B/N)$.
